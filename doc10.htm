The World Wide Web

The Internet is a world wide network of networks, the reason for its existence, to accomodate the sharing of information and communication throughout the globe.  It plays host to a lot of different communication systems and information sources, the biggest and most widely used being the World Wide Web.  The Web has been the main reason for the continuing popularity and success of the Internet...

...but where did it come from?
The Web was first conceived in 1989 by an englishman named Tim Berners-Lee.  He drew on the ideas of Vannevar Bush, who in 1945 wrote an article about a future mechanical device called a Memex, which had many similarities to the Web of today.  Berners-Lee fused these ideas into a workable concept for a global, linked hypertext system, also helping to write the software needed to implement it, and devising the protocols needed to make it a reality.

>> see Berners lee's original proposal for the web <<

The Web software and protocols were first released in 1991, but it didn't really take off until 1993, when Mosaic, a web browser with a Graphical User Interface, was released.  Mosaic vastly improved the usability of the Web, apart from a more contemporary 'look and feel', it allowed graphics and text on the same page for the first time, while also making browser software available to the personal computer.  This led to a 'browser boom', and a huge increase in internet usage, often referred to as the 'explosion of the web'.

The technology behind it
The World Wide Web operates on what is known as Client/Server architecture (see below), using web protocols which govern how information is compiled, sent and received over it.  All documents or files on the Web are written in HTML format, held on Servers connected to the Internet, and are allocated a unique address known as the URL.  To view these documents you use a Browser (a 'client' program, such as Mosaic or Navigator), which retrieves a document from the Server using a protocol such as HTTP or FTP, and displays it on your screen.

The explosion of the Web
Due to its versatility, the web is used for a vast number of purposes, communication, shopping, advertising, entertainment, researching, teaching, filing, and customer support, to mention but a few.  The working world and even people at home are now seeing the advantages in having a 'net presence', it is a cheap and effective means of information distribution, and an extremely quick and cost-effective means of communication.
