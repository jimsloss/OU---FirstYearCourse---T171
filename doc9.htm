Module 2

The Graphical User Interface

Introduction

Computers are now a part of everyday life, not only have they become part of the modern working environment but they have also become a necessary piece of home-use equipment or leisure item for most.

Much of the success of the PC lies in its useability.  In the past using a computer meant using a command-line interface, the computer relying on what you typed in order to perform even the simplest task.  In order to use the computer efficiently you had to be fluent in the computer's language.  There has been no change in the underlying reliance of computers on the use of computer language to perform tasks, the difference is that most computers now have a graphical user interface, with advantages such as icons, mouse interaction, toolbars, menus, help screens and windows, making computers much more user friendly.

The History


The Alto

The first computer to use a graphical interface was the Alto, built at Xerox Parc in 1973, but they decided not to market it. Following a visit to Parc by Steve Jobs in 1979, the first commercial use of the technology (which included the 'desktop' metaphor) came when Apple released the Lisa.  But the Lisa was expensive, so the GUI did not become a viable option for the PC until Microsoft introduced its Windows operating system.  By introducing Windows Microsoft brought an affordable and extremely usable system to the general public, which became the standard and made the GUI standard throughout the industry.

[Timeline of the Graphical User Interface]

The GUI problem

Command-line interfaces often allow greater flexibility and control, they're fast and close to the computer, using few resources, however they can be extremly cryptic and take some time to learn.  The graphical interface takes up more of your computer's resources, making it slower and more problematic, it is also heavily dependant on mouse usage and graphical images, and is therefore not user-friendly for the physically impaired, (although screen-reading technology addresses part of the problem).  The GUI also keeps users away from what is really happening 'underneath the hood'.

The dominance of Windows
Microsoft's Windows operating system has become the dominant standard, mainly due to shrewd marketing and cost rather than physical quality or efficiency.  As a result this has bred contempt and anti-microsoft users, who will not use windows (or any microsoft product) as a matter of principle.

Conclusion
By increasing useability the graphical interface made the computer available to a much wider range of people, encouraging creativity, connectivity and a new way of working.  It undoubtedly effected the popularity and success of the pc, however computers have come on in other ways to, (such as networking and microprocessor developments), therefore the GUI is not the only reason for its success.  There's an ongoing usability debate, and from this it would seem that most users would prefer advancements in synergy between CLI and GUI, in order to promote usability and enhance capability.
