2. The Course Tutor's response on completion of the Activity by the group

OK folks.

This is the summary of what you have been discussing. I have created a short list with the most salient points that, according to you, we should take into consideration when doing online conferencing.

1) Come to a shared understanding or consensus;
2) Be open to each other's arguments;
3) Keep messages short;
4) Use (but not overuse!) emoticons to convey emotions and prevent misunderstandings;
5) Be supportive to others and do not let them feel intimidated;
6) Give plenty of constructive feedback, whether positive or negative, otherwise it is difficult to negotiate agreements;
7) Make good use of quoting previous messages, so as to retain the context of what has gone before;
8) Use the system of majority vote when disagreements occur that cannot be resolved in any other way;
9) Post your message to the appropriate forum;
10) Avoid capital letters at any cost, because they give the impression of "shouting";
11) Take the time to understand the meaning of a message before replying to it;
12) Use conflict in a group as a productive tool;
13) Do not sacrifice your view, in order to reach a consensus. All sides (however isolated they might be) must be taken into consideration, before a balanced agreement can be reached.

So, anybody wants to comment on this?

Paolo

The Netiquette principles that can be pointed out from this text are as follows:





1. The messages were sent directly to the conference, not being addressed to any one person

2. My initial message was opened with an introduction and apologies, starting the message in a good manner.

3. All three messages have a good introduction, but in the third message I have taken a point from the tutors summary, and used part of his message to introduce my response, a brief line being sufficient.

4. The quotes indicated in message 1 have quotation marks, but unfortunately I neglected to ’acknowledge’ the author.

5. Each message has presented their 'interpretation’ and invited further discussion.

6. There is an absence of emoticons at this stage, but although emoticons can be useful in conveying tone or manner, I feel they are unsuitable to unfamiliar recipients.  They are best used when corresponding with users who are familiar with the use of emoticons and whom you are aware will not take offence or mis-understand the use of 'silly little faces'. Using emoticons when corresponding with an unfamiliar recipient might seem patronising.
