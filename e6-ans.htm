>Introduction to Dr. Vannevar Bush as the leading scientist and to this article summarising the paper with: The perfection of these pacific instruments should be the first objective of our scientists as they emerge from their war work. This paper calls for a new relationship between thinking man and the sum of our knowledge.
The paper provides ideas on how technology may be improved, giving possible working examples and explainations of how current methods can be improved or used with other existing methods to a better good. He throws out all sorts of ideas which can now be seen in technology more than 50yrs later. The paper was initially aimed at the people who have the thought, ability and resources to make the changes happen, the ideas he presents are all possible extensions of contemporary working methods.

1

[Asks and answers..] Of what lasting benefit has been man's use of science and of the new instruments which his research brought into existence?  Science has provided the swiftest communication between individuals; it has provided a record of ideas and has enabled man to manipulate and to make extracts from that record so that knowledge evolves and endures throughout the life of a race rather than that of an individual.

[Discusses difficulties in transmitting and reviewing the results of the growing 'mountain of research', pointing out that contemporary methods are outdated.  Also notes that problem with having material reach its intended audience]

The difficulty seems to be, not so much that we publish unduly in view of the extent and variety of present day interests, but rather that publication has been extended far beyond our present ability to make real use of the record. The summation of human experience is being expanded at a prodigious rate, and the means we use for threading through the consequent maze to the momentarily important item is the same as was used in the days of square-rigged ships.

Discusses available technology pointing out 'there are plenty of mechanical aids with which to effect a transformation in scientific records.'

Gives two examples, (Leibnitz inventing a calculating machine 2 centuries before, and Babbage's invention of an arithmetical machine) to demonstrate how technological advancement is also hindered by current economic conditions, and how complexity and unreliability were seen to be synonymous.  Uses this as his foothold to sell advancement, giving examples of how contemporary complex items where then being built which were reliable and how the economy was supporting the construction of such complex items.

End message "The world has arrived at an age of cheap complex devices of great reliability; and something is bound to come of it."

2




Discussion on methods of record storage, examines future possibilities and fits these in with contemporary methods.  Gives some detail of the possible advancements of photography (including dry photography)  making comparisons with television at the time to give possible scenarios of 'how' it could be done.  Also discusses possibilities of microphotography and improvments possible with microfilm. Expands this by examining the possible compression of volume "A library of a million volumes could be compressed into one end of a desk."

<If the human race has produced since the invention of movable type a total record, in the form of magazines, newspapers, books, tracts, advertising blurbs, correspondence, having a volume corresponding to a billion books, the whole affair, assembled and compressed, could be lugged off in a moving van.>  [could this be were Ted Nelson got his idea for Xanadu?]

Mere compression, of course, is not enough; one needs not only to make and store a record but also be able to consult it, and this aspect of the matter comes later. Even the modern great library is not generally consulted; it is nibbled at by a few.

Compression is important, however, when it comes to costs. The material for the microfilm Britannica would cost a nickel, and it could be mailed anywhere for a cent. What would it cost to print a million copies?

3

Continuing with record storage he now moves to voice storage.  Discusses possibility of computers which type when talked to, providing contemporary examples, (Voder at World Fair, Bell Laboratories' Vocoder and the stenotype).

Discusses automatic input of data via cards with punched holes, and complete automation of the system. Also notes that these would prove invaluable to some businesses.

Makes point that future arithmetical machines will be electrical in nature, will perform 100 times faster or more, and will be far more versatile than present commercial machines, so that they may readily be adapted for a wide variety of operations. They will be controlled by a control card or film, they will select their own data and manipulate it in accordance with the instructions thus inserted.  Such machines will have enormous appetites. One of them will take instructions and data from a whole roomful of girls armed with simple key board punches, and will deliver sheets of computed results every few minutes. There will always be plenty of things to compute in the detailed affairs of millions of people doing complicated things.

4

Discussion on the importance of computers dealing with arithmetic functions and on how this should continue to proceed, taking on more advanced mathematices for the scientist and other functions, giving the scientist time to work on matters other than working out calculation. Also briefs on relegating matters other than arithmetic and statistics to the machine.

5

This paragraph discusses the aspect of logic thinking by a computer, the need for building mechanisms for reassurance in advanced logic functions.  He discusses the need for symbolism, (machine talk? ie operating system or computer language?) in order to reduce mathematical transformations to machine processes.

Discusses computer selection needs and methods "Selection devices will soon be speeded up from their present rate of reviewing data at a few hundred a minute. By the use of photocells and microfilm they will survey items at the rate of a thousand a second, and will print out duplicates of those selected."  Also selective selection, the telephone exchange, instead of searching items in turn it starts with the first then second number and so on.."the process could be speeded up if increased speed were economically warranted. If necessary, it could be made extremely fast by substituting thermionic-tube switching for mechanical switching, so that the full selection could be made in one one-hundredth of a second. No one would wish to spend the money necessary to make this change in the telephone system, but the general idea is applicable elsewhere."

Talks about the cash register and possible updates, involving rapid selection, dry photography and distant projection.

6

This is the most important chapter, here Bush discusses several items which are now seen in everyday use.

Selection procedures: indexing, subclass to subclass. It can be in only one place, cumbersome rules as to which path will locate it. Having found one item, one has to emerge from the system and re-enter on a new path.

Selection by association, rather than indexing, "The human mind does not work that way. It operates by association. With one item in its grasp, it snaps instantly to the next that is suggested by the association of thoughts" [hyperlinks?]

"One cannot hope thus to equal the speed and flexibility with which the mind follows an associative trail," [I disagree, either this may be matched/overtaken/improved at some future point or we might see our thoughts controlling what we see on screen, possible not even a screen but some three-dimensional interactive image]

Discussion of the "memex".. a sort of mechanized private file and library. A device in which an individual stores all his books, records, and communications, and which is mechanized so that it may be consulted with exceeding speed and flexibility. It is an enlarged intimate supplement to his memory. [possibly ment as storage of experienced things, things you know on record rather than I lot of things you don't know but are yet to read]

It consists of a desk, and while it can presumably be operated from a distance, it is primarily the piece of furniture at which he works. [personal workstation - desktop?] On the top are slanting translucent screens, on which material can be projected for convenient reading. [Monitor] There is a keyboard, and sets of buttons and levers. Otherwise it looks like an ordinary desk.  In one end is the stored material. The matter of bulk is well taken care of by improved microfilm. [computer memory] Only a small part of the interior of the memex is devoted to storage, the rest to mechanism.

Most of the memex contents are purchased on microfilm ready for insertion. Books of all sorts, pictures, current periodicals, newspapers, are thus obtained and dropped into place. Business correspondence takes the same path. And there is provision for direct entry. On the top of the memex is a transparent platen. On this are placed longhand notes, photographs, memoranda, all sorts of things. When one is in place, the depression of a lever causes it to be photographed onto the next blank space in a section of the memex film, dry photography being employed.

There is, of course, provision for consultation of the record by the usual scheme of indexing. If the user wishes to consult a certain book, he taps its code on the keyboard, and the title page of the book promptly appears before him, projected onto one of his viewing positions. Frequently-used codes are mnemonic, so that he seldom consults his code book; but when he does, a single tap of a key projects it for his use.  [Function keys] Moreover, he has supplemental levers. On deflecting one of these levers to the right he runs through the book before him, each page in turn being projected at a speed which just allows a recognizing glance at each. If he deflects it further to the right, he steps through the book 10 pages at a time; [as on word processor] still further at 100 pages at a time. Deflection to the left gives him the same control backwards.

A special button transfers him immediately to the first page of the index. [Home key] Any given book of his library can thus be called up and consulted with far greater facility than if it were taken from a shelf. As he has several projection positions, he can leave one item in position while he calls up another. [Windows] He can add marginal notes and comments, taking advantage of one possible type of dry photography, and it could even be arranged so that he can do this by a stylus scheme, such as is now employed in the telautograph seen in railroad waiting rooms, just as though he had the physical page before him.

7

Details of the associative indexing, whereby any item may be caused at will to select immediately and automatically another. This is the essential feature of the memex. The process of tying two items together is the important thing.

When the user is building a trail, he names it, inserts the name in his code book, and taps it out on his keyboard. Before him are the two items to be joined, projected onto adjacent viewing positions. At the bottom of each there are a number of blank code spaces, and a pointer is set to indicate one of these on each item. The user taps a single key, and the items are permanently joined. In each code space appears the code word. Out of view, but also in the code space, is inserted a set of dots for photocell viewing; and on each item these dots by their positions designate the index number of the other item. [hypertext working]

Thereafter, at any time, when one of these items is in view, the other can be instantly recalled merely by tapping a button below the corresponding code space. Moreover, when numerous items have been thus joined together to form a trail, they can be reviewed in turn, rapidly or slowly, by deflecting a lever like that used for turning the pages of a book. It is exactly as though the physical items had been gathered together from widely separated sources and bound together to form a new book. It is more than this, for any item can be joined into numerous trails. [there is mention in the course material that this refers to what is now the internet.  looking at this from Bush's perspective I see this as a way of using the memex to view the self-contained messages for the single user.  However the anologies with the internet are astounding considering this was written in 1945, long before the pc.  The amount of memory, the threads, the linking of documents, widely separated sources bound together. The important difference here, which is yet to be established, is that all the documents the user needs are held on the computers memory itself.]

Interesting example of how someone would use the memex in order to obtain information on a given subject, view and link documents to form a trail and inputting his own comments as part of the trail.  There is also mention here of copying the trail in order that it may be passed on to someone else to use in their memex.

8

This section looks at the availability and usages of readymade trails, within the available exterior microfilm sources.

Points out that his views here are based on sticking closely to methods and elements already known and improving, rather than basing his prophecies on the unknown.

Discusses current uses of mechanical items which convey information from the senses, such as the tv operating like the eye, the hearing aid, the typist and the encephalograph (reads brain activity). explains the processes used in these methods and asks " Is it not possible that some day the path may be established more directly?" [ie that the brain signals themselves might induce the control or activity, rather than the transforation of electrical signals to mechanical and back to electrical again.]
